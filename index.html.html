01 What Are PFAS

Man-Made Chemicals Built to Last Forever

Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS) are a group of more than 12,000 synthetic chemicals that have been manufactured and used in industries and consumer products since the 1940s. Their defining feature is a strong carbon-fluorine bond one of the strongest in organic chemistry which makes them virtually indestructible in the natural environment.

PFAS repel water, oil, grease, and heat, making them useful in nonstick pots and pans, water-resistant clothing, food packaging, firefighting foam, and hundreds of industrial processes. But this same durability means they do not break down earning them the nickname "forever chemicals."

They accumulate in soil, groundwater, surface water, wildlife, and the human body. Once they enter a water supply, they are extraordinarily difficult to remove. As of 2025, no single proven technology exists to fully eliminate PFAS contamination from a water system.

02 Health Effects

What PFAS Do to the Human Body

PFAS accumulate in human tissue over time. Research has linked chronic exposure to a wide range of serious health outcomes, with children and pregnant women among the most vulnerable populations.

🧬 High Risk

Cancer

Exposure to PFOA and PFOS has been linked to increased risk of kidney, liver, and testicular cancers, supported by multiple systematic reviews.

🛡️ Documented

Immune Suppression

Children with higher PFAS blood levels show measurably weaker antibody responses after routine vaccinations, leaving them more vulnerable to infectious disease.

🧒 High Risk

Developmental Effects

Prenatal PFAS exposure is associated with low birth weight, altered thyroid hormone levels, and disrupted hormonal development in infants and children.

❤️ Documented

Cardiovascular Disease

Studies have found associations between PFAS exposure and elevated cholesterol, hypertension, and increased risk of cardiovascular events.

🔬 Emerging

Liver Damage

PFAS accumulate in liver tissue and have been associated with liver enzyme abnormalities and non-alcoholic fatty liver disease in exposed populations.

🤰 High Risk

Reproductive Harm

PFAS exposure has been linked to reduced fertility, pregnancy-induced hypertension, and preeclampsia, with effects documented in both men and women.

03 A History of Negligence

Decades of Delay

The story of PFAS is not just a scientific story it is a story of corporate secrecy, regulatory failure, and communities left without answers for generations.

1940s

PFAS Introduced

3M and DuPont begin manufacturing PFAS compounds for industrial and consumer use. Their unique properties make them commercially valuable almost immediately.

1970s–1980s

Internal Studies Show Harm

Internal corporate studies begin documenting health risks of PFAS exposure in workers. These findings are not shared with regulators or the public for decades.

~2000

Kimberly-Clark Allegedly Aware in New Milford, CT

Kimberly-Clark is alleged to have known about PFAS dangers from its New Milford facility by at least this year, yet took no meaningful action to address contamination affecting nearby residents.

2012

Landmark JAMA Study Published

A study in the Journal of the American Medical Association demonstrates that PFAS exposure in children significantly weakens immune responses to vaccines even at levels common in the general population.

2016

EPA Issues Health Advisories

The EPA issues non-enforceable health advisory levels for PFOA and PFOS but stops short of legally binding limits, leaving communities without enforceable protections.

2024

First Federal Drinking Water Limits Finally

The EPA finalizes the first-ever National Primary Drinking Water Regulation for PFAS, setting a maximum contaminant level of 4 parts per trillion for PFOS and PFOA. Communities had been unprotected for over 80 years of industrial use.

2025

No Proven Cleanup Solution Exists

A comprehensive review of 581 peer-reviewed studies concludes that despite progress in treatment technologies, no single proven solution exists to fully eliminate PFAS contamination from water systems.

04 Local Crisis

New Milford, CT: A Community at Risk

The PFAS crisis is not distant. In New Milford, Connecticut, residents living near a Kimberly-Clark facility have faced the consequences of alleged corporate negligence and decades of regulatory inaction.

"Kimberly-Clark allegedly knew about the dangers of PFAS by at least 2000 and took no action to deal with it leaving a community exposed for nearly a quarter century."

PFAS from industrial operations can migrate through soil and into groundwater, contaminating the drinking water of residents who may have no idea the risk exists. Because PFAS are colorless and odorless, there is no way to detect them without laboratory testing.

The New Milford case is a local example of a national pattern: industries using PFAS-containing products near communities, with slow regulatory response and real health consequences for residents who trusted their water was safe.

Residents have the right to know what is in their water. The combination of alleged corporate concealment and the EPA's delayed regulatory action created a situation where that right was denied for decades.

05 What Can Be Done

Addressing the PFAS Crisis

While no great solution exists, researchers, regulators, and communities are pursuing multiple approaches to reduce exposure and begin remediation.

Activated Carbon Filtration

Activated carbon is the EPA's "Best Available Technology" for PFAS removal. It is effective at reducing PFOS and PFOA in drinking water, though less effective for shorter-chain PFAS and is impacted by competing organic matter.

Regulatory Enforcement

The 2024 EPA drinking water rule gives regulators enforceable limits for the first time. Compliance deadlines require water utilities to test for PFAS and take action if levels exceed 4 ppt.

Community Testing & Awareness

Residents near industrial sites can request water testing. Organizations like the Environmental Working Group maintain public maps of known PFAS contamination sites across the U.S.

Corporate Accountability

Legal action against manufacturers like 3M and DuPont has resulted in multi-billion dollar settlements. Ongoing litigation continues to push for cleanup funding and accountability.
